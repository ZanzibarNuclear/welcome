# Carbon-14

Carbon-14 is a radioactive isotope of carbon with 6 protons and 8 neutrons. It forms naturally in the upper atmosphere when cosmic-ray neutrons strike nitrogen-14, transforming it into carbon-14 and a free proton.

## Production and Distribution

Cosmic rays bombard Earth's atmosphere continuously, producing a roughly steady-state concentration of carbon-14. This isotope mixes into the carbon dioxide cycle and enters living organisms through photosynthesis and the food chain. While an organism is alive, it maintains the same ratio of carbon-14 to stable carbon-12 as the atmosphere. When it dies, that exchange stops and carbon-14 begins its slow decay.

## Decay

Carbon-14 undergoes beta decay, emitting an electron and an antineutrino to become nitrogen-14:

**¹⁴C → ¹⁴N + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ**

With a half-life of 5,730 years, after ten half-lives (~57,300 years) less than 0.1% of the original carbon-14 remains — which sets the practical upper limit for radiocarbon dating.

## Radiocarbon Dating

By measuring the ratio of carbon-14 to carbon-12 in a sample and comparing it to the known atmospheric ratio, scientists can calculate how long ago the organism died. The technique works for organic materials up to roughly 50,000 years old and has been fundamental in archaeology, paleoclimatology, and ecology.
