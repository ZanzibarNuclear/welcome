# Isotopes

Every element comes in variants. Atoms of the same element share an atomic number, which means they have the same number of protons. But they can carry different numbers of neutrons. These variants are called isotopes.

Isotopes range from perfectly stable to radioactive, from clinically useful to cosmically abundant. Understanding them helps explain atomic mass, radiation, radiocarbon dating, nuclear medicine, and the behavior of reactor fuel.

## Protons Define the Element

The number of protons determines what element an atom is. Carbon always has 6 protons. Uranium always has 92 protons. If the proton count changes, the atom becomes a different element.

## Neutrons Define the Isotope

Neutrons add mass and influence nuclear stability. Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 are both carbon because they both have 6 protons. Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons, while Carbon-14 has 8 neutrons.

The shorthand name combines the element name with the total number of protons and neutrons:

- Carbon-12: 6 protons + 6 neutrons
- Carbon-14: 6 protons + 8 neutrons
- Uranium-235: 92 protons + 143 neutrons

## Stable and Radioactive Isotopes

Some isotope combinations are stable. Others are unstable and eventually decay, releasing particles or energy as the nucleus changes into a more stable arrangement.

Radioactive isotopes are not all the same. Some decay in fractions of a second. Others persist for thousands, millions, or billions of years.

## Example: Carbon-14

Carbon-14 is a radioactive isotope of carbon with 6 protons and 8 neutrons. It forms naturally in the upper atmosphere when cosmic-ray neutrons strike nitrogen-14, transforming it into carbon-14 and a free proton.

Cosmic rays produce a roughly steady concentration of Carbon-14 in the atmosphere. This isotope mixes into the carbon dioxide cycle and enters living organisms through photosynthesis and the food chain.

While an organism is alive, it maintains the same ratio of Carbon-14 to stable Carbon-12 as the atmosphere. When it dies, that exchange stops and Carbon-14 begins its slow decay.

## Radiocarbon Dating

Carbon-14 undergoes beta decay, becoming nitrogen-14:

**Carbon-14 -> Nitrogen-14 + electron + antineutrino**

With a half-life of about 5,730 years, Carbon-14 is useful for dating organic materials up to roughly 50,000 years old. By measuring the ratio of Carbon-14 to Carbon-12 in a sample, scientists can estimate how long ago the organism died.

## Why Isotopes Matter

Isotopes show up throughout science and technology:

- Radiocarbon dating of archaeological and geological samples
- Medical imaging and tracer studies
- Nuclear reactor fuel and waste products
- Climate and atmospheric carbon cycle research
- Understanding how elements form in stars

The key idea is simple: the element is set by protons, but the isotope is shaped by neutrons.
